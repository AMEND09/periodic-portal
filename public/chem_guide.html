General Chemistry Concepts

This section covers fundamental concepts in chemistry, including the states of matter, naming conventions for ions and compounds, an overview of the periodic table classifications, and basic principles like significant figures and density calculations. Understanding these basics is crucial for tackling more complex topics.

  • Solid: Definite shape; definite volume.
  • Liquid: Indefinite shape; definite volume.
  • Gas: Indefinite shape; indefinite volume.
  • Cation (Positive Ion): Element name + "ion" (e.g., Na$^+$: Sodium ion).
  • Anion (Negative Ion): Change element ending to "ide" (e.g., Cl$^- $: Chloride).
  • Multivalent Ions: Use Roman numerals in parentheses after the element name to indicate the charge (e.g., Chromium(II) iodide for CrI$_2$). Latin affixes (-ic for higher charge, -ous for lower charge) can also be used.
  • Ionic Compounds: Name the cation first, then the anion (dropping "ion" from the anion's name). For polyatomic ions, name the cation then the polyatomic ion. Example: NaCl is Sodium chloride. CaSO$_4$ is Calcium sulfate.
  • Covalent Compounds (Molecules): Use prefixes to indicate the number of atoms of each element. Both elements in the compound need a prefix unless the first one is "mono-". Example: H$_2$O is Dihydrogen monoxide. CO$_2$ is Carbon dioxide.

These are all Changes of State:

  • Melting (Solid $\rightarrow$ Liquid): Requires energy (endothermic).
  • Vaporizing (Liquid $\rightarrow$ Gas): Requires energy (endothermic). This includes boiling (rapid vaporization throughout the liquid at a specific temperature) and evaporation (slower vaporization at the surface below the boiling point).
  • Freezing (Liquid $\rightarrow$ Solid): Releases energy (exothermic).
  • Condensation (Gas $\rightarrow$ Liquid): Releases energy (exothermic).
  • Sublimation (Solid $\rightarrow$ Gas): Requires energy (endothermic).
  • Deposition (Gas $\rightarrow$ Solid): Releases energy (exothermic).
  • Physical Change: Alters physical properties of matter without changing its chemical identity (e.g., cutting, changing of state, dissolving).
  • Chemical Change: Alters chemical properties of matter, resulting in a new substance with a new chemical identity (e.g., burning, cooking, rusting). Indicators include energy change (heat, light), color change, gas formation, and precipitate formation.
  • Multiplying and Dividing: The answer will have the same number of significant figures as the value with the least significant figures in the operation. Round at the end.
  • Adding or Subtracting: The answer will have the same number of digits to the right of the decimal point as the value with the least digits to the right of the decimal point in the operation. Round at the end.

Use the formula: $D = m/v$ (Density = mass / volume). The notes also provide rearranged forms: $m = Dv$ and $v = m/D$.

The notes explicitly mention: Goggles/Safety Glasses should be worn to protect eyes, worn over prescription glasses, and contact lenses should not be worn in the lab. Proper handling of lab equipment is also implied. General lab safety includes tying back long hair, wearing closed-toe shoes, no eating or drinking, and knowing the location of safety equipment.

Atomic Structure & The Atom

This section delves into the fundamental building block of matter: the atom. Explore the historical development of atomic theory, the subatomic particles (protons, neutrons, electrons), and concepts like atomic number, mass number, isotopes, and ions. Understanding electron configurations is also key here.

  • Atomic Number (Z): The number of protons in the nucleus of an atom. It uniquely identifies an element.
  • Mass Number (A): The total number of protons and neutrons in an atom's nucleus.
  • Protons: Equal to the atomic number (Z).
  • Neutrons: Mass number (A) - Atomic number (Z).
  • Electrons:
    • For a neutral atom: Equal to the number of protons.
    • For an ion: Number of protons - charge (for cations, e.g., if charge is +2, subtract 2 electrons) or Number of protons + |charge| (for anions, e.g., if charge is -1, add 1 electron).
  • Isotope: Atoms of the same element (same number of protons) that have different numbers of neutrons, and therefore different mass numbers. They are chemically alike but differ in physical properties.
  • Ion: An atom or molecule that has gained or lost one or more electrons, giving it a net positive or negative electrical charge.
    • Cation: Positive ion (lost electrons).
    • Anion: Negative ion (gained electrons).

How to do electron configurations: Follow these principles:

  • Aufbau Principle: Electrons fill the lowest available energy levels (orbitals) first.
  • Pauli Exclusion Principle: A maximum of two electrons can occupy a single atomic orbital, and these two electrons must have opposite spins.
  • Hund's Rule: Electrons will individually occupy each orbital within a subshell before doubling up in any one orbital. When occupying orbitals of equal energy, electrons will have parallel spins.

In the configuration [Ne] 3s$^2$3p$^3$:

  • [Ne]: Represents the electron configuration of Neon, the preceding noble gas (noble gas shorthand).
  • Coefficients (large numbers like 3): Represent the principal energy level (shell).
  • Letters (s, p): Represent the type of sublevel (orbital shape).
  • Superscripts (small numbers like 2 and 3): Represent the number of electrons in that specific sublevel.
  • John Dalton: Developed the first comprehensive atomic theory, proposing that all matter is made of indivisible atoms, atoms of the same element are identical, atoms of different elements are different, atoms combine in simple whole-number ratios to form compounds, and atoms are rearranged in chemical reactions.
  • J.J. Thomson: Discovered the electron using cathode ray tubes. He determined the charge-to-mass ratio of electrons and proposed the "Plum Pudding Model" of the atom, where negatively charged electrons were embedded within a positively charged sphere.
  • Ernest Rutherford: Conducted the Gold Foil Experiment, which disproved the Plum Pudding Model. His findings led to the nuclear model of the atom, proposing that atoms have a small, dense, positively charged nucleus at the center, with electrons orbiting it.

Periodic Table & Trends

The periodic table is a cornerstone of chemistry, organizing elements based on their properties. This section covers the structure of the table (groups, periods, blocks), classifications of elements (metals, non-metals, specific families), and key periodic trends such as atomic radius, ionization energy, and electronegativity. Visualizing these trends can greatly aid understanding.

  • Alkali metals: Group 1
  • Alkaline Earth metals: Group 2 (from notes)
  • Transition metals: Groups 3–12
  • Inner transition metals: Lanthanides and Actinides (usually shown below the main table, technically part of groups 3-12 periods 6 and 7)
  • Halogens: Group 17
  • Noble gases: Group 18 (also called Inert Gases)
  • Gases at room temperature: Noble gases, hydrogen, nitrogen, oxygen, fluorine, and chlorine.
  • Liquids at room temperature: Mercury (Hg) and bromine (Br).
  • Solids at room temperature: Most elements.
  • Man-made elements: All elements above atomic number 92 (transuranium elements), plus technetium (Tc, atomic number 43) and promethium (Pm, atomic number 61).

Atomic radius generally:

  • Increases down a group: More energy levels are added, so the outermost electrons are further from the nucleus.
  • Decreases across a period (left to right): The number of protons in the nucleus increases, leading to a stronger effective nuclear charge that pulls the electrons closer to the nucleus, despite being in the same energy level.

Electronegativity (the tendency of an atom to attract electrons in a chemical bond) generally:

  • Increases across a period (left to right): Atoms become smaller and the nuclear charge increases, making it easier to attract bonding electrons.
  • Decreases down a group: Atoms become larger, and the bonding electrons are further from the nucleus and shielded by more inner electrons, reducing the attraction.
  • Noble gases generally have no electronegativity values as they typically do not form bonds.

Ionization energy (the energy required to remove an electron from a gaseous atom or ion) generally:

  • Increases across a period (left to right): Atoms become smaller and the effective nuclear charge increases, holding electrons more tightly.
  • Decreases down a group: The outermost electron is further from the nucleus and shielded by inner electrons, making it easier to remove.

Elements in the same group (column) of the periodic table have similar chemical properties because they have the same number of valence electrons (electrons in the outermost energy level). Valence electrons are primarily responsible for an atom's chemical behavior and how it bonds with other atoms.

The periodic table is divided into blocks based on the sublevel being filled with valence electrons:

  • s-block: Groups 1 and 2 (Alkali Metals and Alkaline Earth Metals), plus Helium. Their valence electrons are in the s sublevel.
  • p-block: Groups 13 to 18 (excluding Helium). Their valence electrons are in the p sublevel (along with the s sublevel of the same energy level).
  • d-block: Groups 3 to 12 (Transition Metals). Their valence electrons are in the d sublevel of the energy level below the outermost s sublevel.
  • f-block: Lanthanides and Actinides (Inner Transition Metals), usually placed below the main table. Their valence electrons are in the f sublevel, typically two energy levels below the outermost s sublevel.

Chemical Bonding & Molecular Structure

This section explores how atoms connect to form molecules and compounds. Learn about the main types of chemical bonds (ionic and covalent), how to name compounds based on their bonding, the concept of polarity, and how VSEPR theory helps predict molecular shapes. Lewis dot structures are a key tool for visualizing bonding.

  • Ionic Bond: Involves the complete transfer of electrons from one atom (usually a metal) to another (usually a nonmetal), forming oppositely charged ions (cations and anions) that are held together by electrostatic attraction.
  • Covalent Bond: Involves the sharing of electrons between two atoms (typically nonmetals) to achieve a stable electron configuration (often an octet).

Bond Polarity:

  • Non-Polar Covalent Bond: Electrons are shared equally between two atoms. This occurs when the two atoms have very similar or identical electronegativities (difference typically 0.0 – 0.4).
  • Polar Covalent Bond: Electrons are shared unequally between two atoms due to a significant difference in their electronegativities (difference typically >0.4 – 2.0). The atom with higher electronegativity pulls the shared electrons closer, creating a partial negative charge ($\delta$-) on that atom and a partial positive charge ($\delta$+) on the other.

Molecule Polarity:

A molecule is polar (a dipole) if it meets two conditions:

  1. It must contain polar bonds.
  2. The molecular shape must be asymmetrical, such that the bond dipoles do not cancel each other out. Symmetrical molecules with polar bonds can be nonpolar if the dipoles cancel (e.g., CO$_2$).

Example: H$_2$O is polar (bent shape, asymmetrical arrangement of polar O-H bonds). CO$_2$ is nonpolar (linear shape, symmetrical arrangement of polar C=O bonds, dipoles cancel).

VSEPR stands for Valence Shell Electron Pair Repulsion theory. It states that electron pairs (both bonding pairs and lone pairs) around a central atom will arrange themselves as far apart as possible to minimize repulsion, thus determining the molecule's shape.

Common molecular shapes and their ideal bond angles include:

  • Linear: 2 bonding groups, 0 lone pairs. Bond angle: 180$^\circ$. Example: CO$_2$, HCN.
  • Trigonal Planar: 3 bonding groups, 0 lone pairs. Bond angle: 120$^\circ$. Example: BCl$_3$.
  • Tetrahedral: 4 bonding groups, 0 lone pairs. Bond angle: 109.5$^\circ$. Example: CH$_4$.
  • Trigonal Pyramidal: 3 bonding groups, 1 lone pair. Bond angle: <109.5$^\circ$ (approx. 107$^\circ$). Example: NH$_3$.
  • Bent (or Angular): 2 bonding groups, 2 lone pairs (from tetrahedral electron geometry). Bond angle: <109.5$^\circ$ (approx. 104.5$^\circ$ for H$_2$O). Example: H$_2$O.
  • Bent (or Angular): 2 bonding groups, 1 lone pair (from trigonal planar electron geometry). Bond angle: <120$^\circ$. Example: SO$_2$.

(The notes provide a more extensive table with shapes like Trigonal Bipyramidal, Octahedral, etc.)

For a Neutral Element:

  1. Write the chemical symbol of the element.
  2. Determine the number of valence electrons (usually based on its group number for representative elements).
  3. Place dots around the symbol, one at a time on each of the four sides, before pairing them up. Each dot represents one valence electron.

Example: Oxygen (O) is in Group 16, so it has 6 valence electrons. Its Lewis dot structure would have the symbol O with 6 dots around it (two pairs and two single dots).

For an Ion:

  1. Determine the total number of valence electrons for the atoms involved.
  2. Add electrons for negative charges or subtract electrons for positive charges.
  3. Arrange the atoms with the least electronegative atom usually in the center (hydrogen is always terminal).
  4. Connect atoms with single bonds (one shared pair of electrons).
  5. Distribute the remaining electrons as lone pairs to satisfy the octet rule (or duet rule for hydrogen) for terminal atoms first, then the central atom.
  6. If the central atom does not have an octet, form multiple bonds (double or triple) by moving lone pairs from terminal atoms.
  7. Enclose the entire structure in square brackets and write the charge as a superscript outside the brackets.

Example: Ammonium ion (NH$_4$$^+$). N has 5 valence e$^-$, each H has 1 (total 4). Positive charge means subtract 1 e$^-$. Total = 5 + 4 - 1 = 8 valence e$^-$. N is central, bonded to 4 H atoms. Each bond uses 2 e$^-$, so 4 bonds use 8 e$^-$. N has an octet, each H has a duet. Structure: [H-N(H)(H)-H]$^+$ (with N having single bonds to each H).

Chemical Reactions

Chemical reactions involve the transformation of substances into new ones. This section covers the five main types of chemical reactions (Synthesis, Decomposition, Single Displacement, Double Displacement, Combustion), how to predict their products, balance chemical equations, and understand concepts like oxidation-reduction (redox) reactions, activity series, and solubility rules.

  1. Synthesis (Combination): Two or more simple substances combine to form a more complex substance. A + B $\rightarrow$ AB.
    Example: $CaO(s) + H_2O(l) \rightarrow Ca(OH)_2(aq)$ (Metallic oxide + water $\rightarrow$ Base)
  2. Decomposition: A complex substance breaks down into two or more simpler substances. AB $\rightarrow$ A + B.
    Example: $2H_2O(l) \rightarrow 2H_2(g) + O_2(g)$ (Binary compounds $\rightarrow$ elements)
  3. Single Displacement (Replacement): One element replaces another element in a compound. A + BC $\rightarrow$ AC + B.
    Example: $Mg(s) + 2AgNO_3(aq) \rightarrow Mg(NO_3)_2(aq) + 2Ag(s)$ (Requires activity series to predict if reaction occurs)
  4. Double Displacement (Metathesis): The ions of two compounds exchange places in an aqueous solution to form two new compounds. AB + CD $\rightarrow$ AD + CB.
    Example: $Al_2(SO_4)_3(aq) + 6NaOH(aq) \rightarrow 2Al(OH)_3(s) + 3Na_2SO_4(aq)$ (Often forms a precipitate, gas, or water)
  5. Combustion: A substance reacts rapidly with oxygen (O$_2$), often producing heat and light. Typically involves hydrocarbons or organic compounds reacting with oxygen to produce carbon dioxide (CO$_2$) and water (H$_2$O).
    Example: $C_3H_8(g) + 5O_2(g) \rightarrow 3CO_2(g) + 4H_2O(g)$ (Propane combustion)

In a redox (oxidation-reduction) reaction:

  • Oxidation: An element is oxidized if its oxidation number increases. This corresponds to a loss of electrons (OIL - Oxidation Is Loss).
  • Reduction: An element is reduced if its oxidation number decreases. This corresponds to a gain of electrons (RIG - Reduction Is Gain).

Rules for Assigning Oxidation Numbers:

  1. An uncombined element (e.g., O$_2$, Fe) = 0.
  2. A monatomic ion = Charge on the ion (e.g., Na$^+$ = +1, Cl$^- $ = -1).
  3. Fluorine in compounds = –1.
  4. Oxygen in compounds = –2 (Exceptions: in peroxides like H$_2$O$_2$, O = –1; in compounds with fluorine, O can be positive).
  5. Hydrogen in compounds = +1 (Exception: in metal hydrides like NaH, H = –1).
  6. Group 1 metals in compounds = +1.
  7. Group 2 metals in compounds = +2.
  8. Aluminum in compounds = +3.
  9. The sum of oxidation numbers in a neutral compound = 0.
  10. The sum of oxidation numbers in a polyatomic ion = Charge on the ion.

Activity Series:

The activity series (for metals or halogens) is used to predict whether a single displacement reaction will occur. A more reactive element (higher on the activity series) will displace a less reactive element from its compound. If the free element is less reactive than the element in the compound, no reaction occurs.

Solubility Rules:

Solubility rules are used to predict whether a precipitate (an insoluble solid) will form in a double displacement reaction occurring in an aqueous solution. After predicting the potential products, you consult the solubility rules (like Table I in the notes) to determine if either product is insoluble in water. If an insoluble product is formed, it is written with an (s) state symbol, indicating a precipitate.

Stoichiometry

Stoichiometry is the quantitative study of reactants and products in chemical reactions. This section covers mole conversions (mass, volume at STP, particles), calculating percent composition, determining empirical and molecular formulas, and solving problems involving limiting reactants and percent yield. Mastering these calculations is essential for practical chemistry.

To solve this, you use the "Mass $\leftrightarrow$ Mole" conversion, using the molar mass of lithium bromide (LiBr).

  1. Find the molar mass of LiBr:
    • Li: 6.941 g/mol
    • Br: 79.904 g/mol
    • Molar Mass of LiBr = 6.941 + 79.904 = 86.845 g/mol
  2. Calculate moles:
    Moles = Mass / Molar Mass
    Moles = 97.8 g / 86.845 g/mol = 1.126 mol LiBr (approx.)

Steps:

  1. Convert grams of each element to moles:
    • Moles Br = 46.75 g / 79.904 g/mol (molar mass of Br) = 0.5850 mol Br
    • Moles C = 1.75 g / 12.011 g/mol (molar mass of C) = 0.1457 mol C
  2. Divide the mole values by the smallest number of moles (0.1457 mol in this case):
    • For Br: 0.5850 mol / 0.1457 mol $\approx$ 4.015 $\approx$ 4
    • For C: 0.1457 mol / 0.1457 mol = 1
  3. The resulting numbers are the subscripts in the empirical formula.

Empirical Formula: CBr$_4$

Step 1: Determine the Empirical Formula

  1. Assume a 100 g sample. This means you have:
    • 40.0 g Carbon (C)
    • 6.7 g Hydrogen (H)
    • 53.3 g Oxygen (O)
  2. Convert grams to moles for each element:
    • Moles C = 40.0 g / 12.011 g/mol = 3.330 mol C
    • Moles H = 6.7 g / 1.008 g/mol = 6.647 mol H
    • Moles O = 53.3 g / 15.999 g/mol = 3.331 mol O
  3. Divide each mole value by the smallest number of moles (approx. 3.330 mol):
    • C: 3.330 / 3.330 = 1
    • H: 6.647 / 3.330 $\approx$ 2
    • O: 3.331 / 3.330 $\approx$ 1
  4. The empirical formula is CH$_2$O.

Step 2: Determine the Molecular Formula

  1. Calculate the molar mass of the empirical formula (CH$_2$O):
    Molar Mass (CH$_2$O) = 12.011 (C) + 2 * 1.008 (H) + 15.999 (O) = 30.026 g/mol.
  2. Divide the given molar mass of the compound by the empirical formula mass:
    Ratio = (Molar Mass of Compound) / (Empirical Formula Mass)
    Ratio = 60 g/mol / 30.026 g/mol $\approx$ 2
  3. Multiply the subscripts in the empirical formula by this ratio (2):
    Molecular Formula = (CH$_2$O)$_2$ = C$_2$H$_4$O$_2$

Molecular Formula: C$_2$H$_4$O$_2$

States of Matter & Intermolecular Forces

This section examines the different states of matter (solid, liquid, gas) and the forces that exist between molecules, known as intermolecular forces (IMFs). Understanding IMFs like London dispersion forces, dipole-dipole interactions, and hydrogen bonding is key to explaining many physical properties of substances, such as boiling points and viscosity.

Intermolecular forces (IMFs) are attractions between molecules. Their relative strengths are generally:

  1. Ion-Dipole Forces: Strongest among those listed if an ion is involved. Attraction between an ion and a polar molecule. (e.g., NaCl dissolved in H$_2$O).
  2. Hydrogen Bonding: A special, strong type of dipole-dipole interaction. Occurs when hydrogen is bonded to a highly electronegative atom (Nitrogen, Oxygen, or Fluorine - N, O, F) and is attracted to an N, O, or F atom in a nearby molecule. (e.g., between H$_2$O molecules).
  3. Dipole-Dipole Forces: Attraction between polar molecules (molecules with permanent dipoles). Weaker than hydrogen bonds. (e.g., between HCl molecules).
  4. London Dispersion Forces (LDF): Weakest type of IMF. Present in ALL molecules (polar and nonpolar). Caused by temporary, induced dipoles due to the constant motion of electrons. Strength increases with the size of the molecule (more electrons means greater polarizability).

So, in general: Ion-Dipole > Hydrogen Bonding > Dipole-Dipole > London Dispersion Forces.

Solutions & Concentrations

Solutions are homogeneous mixtures. This section focuses on understanding solutes and solvents, different ways to express concentration (molarity, molality), the dilution process, and colligative properties like freezing point depression and boiling point elevation, which depend on the concentration of solute particles.

Molarity (M): Moles of solute per liter of solution.

$M = \frac{\text{moles of solute}}{\text{Liters of solution}}$

Example from notes: If you need to make 375 mL of 1.2 M sodium hydroxide (NaOH), you need 18 grams of NaOH. (Calculation: 1.2 mol/L * 0.375 L = 0.45 mol NaOH; 0.45 mol * 40.00 g/mol NaOH = 18 g NaOH).

Molality (m): Moles of solute per kilogram of solvent.

$m = \frac{\text{moles of solute}}{\text{kilograms of solvent}}$

Example from notes: Calculate molality of 422 grams of sodium chloride (NaCl) diluted in 475 mL H$_2$O (density of water $\approx$ 1 g/mL, so 475 mL H$_2$O $\approx$ 0.475 kg H$_2$O).
Moles NaCl = 422 g / 58.44 g/mol = 7.22 mol NaCl.
Molality = 7.22 mol / 0.475 kg = 15.2 m NaCl (approx.).

Freezing Point Depression: The presence of a solute lowers the freezing point of a solvent.

$\Delta T_f = i \cdot K_f \cdot m$

  • $\Delta T_f$: Change in freezing point (T$_{f,\text{solvent}}$ - T$_{f,\text{solution}}$).
  • $i$: van 't Hoff factor (number of particles the solute dissociates into; i=1 for non-electrolytes).
  • $K_f$: Freezing point depression constant of the solvent (for water, $K_f$ = 1.86 $^\circ$C$\cdot$kg/mol).
  • $m$: Molality of the solution.

Boiling Point Elevation: The presence of a solute raises the boiling point of a solvent.

$\Delta T_b = i \cdot K_b \cdot m$

  • $\Delta T_b$: Change in boiling point (T$_{b,\text{solution}}$ - T$_{b,\text{solvent}}$).
  • $i$: van 't Hoff factor.
  • $K_b$: Boiling point elevation constant of the solvent (for water, $K_b$ = 0.512 $^\circ$C$\cdot$kg/mol).
  • $m$: Molality of the solution.

The notes provide examples for C$_6$H$_{12}$O$_6$ (a non-electrolyte, so i=1) and NaCl (an electrolyte, i=2 as it dissociates into Na$^+$ and Cl$^- $).

Acids, Bases & pH

This section covers the properties and definitions of acids and bases (Arrhenius, Brønsted-Lowry), the concept of pH and pOH for measuring acidity/basicity, strong vs. weak acids/bases, and acid-base neutralization reactions including titrations. Understanding conjugate acid-base pairs is also important.

Arrhenius Definition:

  • Arrhenius Acid: A substance that generates H$^+$ ions (protons) when dissolved in water. (e.g., HCl, CH$_3$COOH).
  • Arrhenius Base: A substance that generates OH$^- $ ions (hydroxide ions) when dissolved in water. (e.g., NaOH).

Brønsted-Lowry Definition:

  • Brønsted-Lowry Acid: A proton (H$^+$) donor.
  • Brønsted-Lowry Base: A proton (H$^+$) acceptor. (This definition is broader; bases do not need to contain OH$^- $, e.g., NH$_3$).

Key relationships at 25$^\circ$C:

  • Ion product of water: $K_w = [\text{H}^+][\text{OH}^-] = 1.0 \times 10^{-14}$
  • pH definition: $pH = -\log[\text{H}^+]$
  • pOH definition: $pOH = -\log[\text{OH}^-]$
  • Relationship between pH and pOH: $pH + pOH = 14$

From these, you can calculate any of the four values if one is known:

  • Given $[\text{H}^+]$, find pH: $pH = -\log[\text{H}^+]$
  • Given pH, find $[\text{H}^+]$: $[\text{H}^+] = 10^{-pH}$
  • Given $[\text{OH}^-]$, find pOH: $pOH = -\log[\text{OH}^-]$
  • Given pOH, find $[\text{OH}^-]$: $[\text{OH}^-] = 10^{-pOH}$
  • Given $[\text{H}^+]$, find $[\text{OH}^-]$: $[\text{OH}^-] = K_w / [\text{H}^+] = (1.0 \times 10^{-14}) / [\text{H}^+]$
  • Given $[\text{OH}^-]$, find $[\text{H}^+]$: $[\text{H}^+] = K_w / [\text{OH}^-] = (1.0 \times 10^{-14}) / [\text{OH}^-]$
  • Given pH, find pOH: $pOH = 14 - pH$
  • Given pOH, find pH: $pH = 14 - pOH$

Thermodynamics & Kinetics

Thermodynamics deals with energy changes in chemical reactions (enthalpy, Hess's Law, calorimetry), while kinetics studies the rates and mechanisms of reactions (collision theory, activation energy, factors affecting reaction rate). This section explores these concepts, helping you understand why and how fast reactions occur.

  • Endothermic Reaction: A reaction that absorbs heat energy from its surroundings. The enthalpy change ($\Delta H$) for an endothermic reaction is positive ($\Delta H > 0$). The products have higher enthalpy than the reactants.
  • Exothermic Reaction: A reaction that releases heat energy into its surroundings. The enthalpy change ($\Delta H$) for an exothermic reaction is negative ($\Delta H < 0$). The products have lower enthalpy than the reactants.

Collision Theory states that for a chemical reaction to occur, reactant particles (atoms, ions, or molecules) must collide with each other.

However, not all collisions result in a reaction. For a collision to be effective (i.e., lead to the formation of products), two main factors must be met:

  1. Sufficient Energy: The colliding particles must possess a minimum amount of kinetic energy, known as the activation energy ($E_a$). This energy is needed to break existing bonds and allow new bonds to form.
  2. Correct Orientation: The colliding particles must be oriented in space in a way that allows the necessary bonds to break and form. If they collide with an improper orientation, they will likely just bounce off each other, even if they have sufficient energy.

Gases & Gas Laws

Gases have unique properties due to the large spaces between particles and their constant motion. This section covers the Kinetic-Molecular Theory (KMT) of gases and various gas laws (Boyle's, Charles's, Gay-Lussac's, Combined, Ideal, Dalton's Law of Partial Pressures, Graham's Law) that describe the relationships between pressure, volume, temperature, and amount of gas.

The Kinetic-Molecular Theory (KMT) of gases describes the behavior of ideal gases based on the following assumptions:

  1. Particle Size and Spacing: Gas molecules are considered to be small particles with negligible volume compared to the large empty spaces between them.
  2. Constant, Random Motion: Gas molecules are in constant, rapid, and random motion, moving in straight lines until they collide with other particles or the walls of their container.
  3. Collisions: Collisions between gas molecules and between molecules and the container walls are perfectly elastic. This means that kinetic energy is conserved during collisions (though it can be transferred between particles).
  4. Negligible Intermolecular Forces: There are essentially no attractive or repulsive forces between gas molecules. They act independently of one another.
  5. Temperature and Kinetic Energy: The average kinetic energy of gas molecules is directly proportional to the absolute temperature (in Kelvin) of the gas. At a given temperature, all gases have the same average kinetic energy.

These laws describe the relationships between pressure (P), volume (V), temperature (T in Kelvin), and the amount of gas (n in moles).

  • Boyle's Law (constant T and n): $P_1V_1 = P_2V_2$. Pressure and volume are inversely proportional.
  • Charles's Law (constant P and n): $\frac{V_1}{T_1} = \frac{V_2}{T_2}$. Volume and absolute temperature are directly proportional.
  • Gay-Lussac's Law (constant V and n): $\frac{P_1}{T_1} = \frac{P_2}{T_2}$. Pressure and absolute temperature are directly proportional.
  • Combined Gas Law (constant n): $\frac{P_1V_1}{T_1} = \frac{P_2V_2}{T_2}$. Combines Boyle's, Charles's, and Gay-Lussac's laws.
  • Ideal Gas Law: $PV = nRT$. Relates all four variables. R is the ideal gas constant (e.g., 0.0821 L$\cdot$atm/mol$\cdot$K or 8.314 J/mol$\cdot$K, depending on units of P and V).
